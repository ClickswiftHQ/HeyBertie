<h2>Why Pricing Matters</h2>
<p>Getting your pricing right is one of the most important decisions you'll make as a groomer. Price too low and you'll burn out working long hours for little profit. Price too high without the reputation to back it up and you'll struggle to fill your diary.</p>
<p>This guide gives you a practical framework to find the sweet spot.</p>

<h2>Step 1: Calculate Your Costs</h2>
<p>Before you can set profitable prices, you need to know what it costs you to run each appointment. Add up your monthly expenses:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Fixed costs</strong> — Rent, insurance, loan repayments, software subscriptions</li>
<li><strong>Variable costs</strong> — Shampoo, blades, electricity, water</li>
<li><strong>Your salary</strong> — What you need to earn to live comfortably</li>
</ul>

<h3>The Simple Formula</h3>
<p>Divide your total monthly costs by the number of dogs you can realistically groom in a month. This gives you your break-even price per dog. Your actual price should be at least 30% above this to account for quiet periods, holidays, and profit.</p>

<h2>Step 2: Research Your Market</h2>
<p>Look at what other groomers in your area charge. Check their websites, heyBertie profiles, and social media. Note down:</p>
<ul>
<li>Their price range for small, medium, and large dogs</li>
<li>What's included in their standard groom</li>
<li>Any add-on services they offer</li>
<li>Their experience level and qualifications</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 3: Structure Your Price List</h2>
<p>Most successful groomers use a tiered pricing structure based on dog size:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Small dogs</strong> (e.g., Shih Tzu, Bichon) — Your base price</li>
<li><strong>Medium dogs</strong> (e.g., Cocker Spaniel, Border Collie) — Base + 25-40%</li>
<li><strong>Large dogs</strong> (e.g., Golden Retriever, Labradoodle) — Base + 50-75%</li>
<li><strong>Giant breeds</strong> (e.g., Newfoundland, St Bernard) — Base + 100%+</li>
</ul>

<h3>Add-On Services</h3>
<p>Offering add-ons increases your average booking value without requiring more time slots:</p>
<ul>
<li>Teeth brushing</li>
<li>De-matting (charged per 15 minutes)</li>
<li>Nail painting</li>
<li>Blueberry facial</li>
<li>Flea treatment</li>
</ul>

<h2>Step 4: Account for Condition Charges</h2>
<p>Dogs that arrive matted, nervous, or with very overgrown coats require extra time and skill. Make sure your pricing accounts for this:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Matting surcharge</strong> — 20-50% extra depending on severity</li>
<li><strong>First-time/nervous dog surcharge</strong> — 10-20% extra for the additional time needed</li>
<li><strong>Overdue coat surcharge</strong> — For dogs that haven't been groomed in 6+ months</li>
</ul>
<p>Be transparent about these charges on your booking page and in your consultation.</p>

<h2>Step 5: Review and Adjust</h2>
<p>Your prices aren't set in stone. Review them every 6 months and consider raising them if:</p>
<ul>
<li>Your diary is consistently full (you're in high demand)</li>
<li>Your costs have increased</li>
<li>You've gained new qualifications or skills</li>
<li>You're regularly turning away clients</li>
</ul>
<p>Give existing clients at least 4 weeks' notice of any price increases, and frame it as a reflection of the quality service you provide.</p>
